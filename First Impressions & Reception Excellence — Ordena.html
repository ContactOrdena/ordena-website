First Impressions & Reception Excellence

30–40 minutes 0.5 hr verifiable CPD All front-of-house roles · Any sector
Introduction

More than answering phones

You are the first person someone meets when they walk through the door. The last person they speak to on the way out. The voice they hear when they're nervous. The face they see when they've had a difficult journey and they're already running late.

What happens at your desk sets the tone for everything that follows.

This module is about doing that part of the job really well — and understanding why it matters more than most people realise.

Section 1

The reality of first impressions

Here's something worth knowing.

7
seconds to form a first impression — before you've said a single useful thing
7×
positive interactions needed to reverse a single negative first impression

Before you've checked someone in, answered their question, or told them where to go — they've already decided how they feel about being there.

Once that impression forms, it sticks. That's a lot of ground to make up from one difficult morning.

But here's the other side of that.

You have the ability to genuinely make someone's day better. Not in a grand way — in a small, human, completely achievable way. The person walking through your door might be nervous, stressed, exhausted, or dealing with something you know nothing about. A warm acknowledgement, a genuine smile, a moment of real attention — these things land. People remember them. They go home and mention them.

That's not a small thing. That's the job.

Section 2

You don't know what they're carrying

This is probably the most important idea in this entire module.

Every person who walks through your door is carrying something you can't see.

Someone coming to see a solicitor might be dealing with a bereavement, a divorce, or a dispute that has kept them awake for weeks. Someone arriving at an accountancy firm might be terrified they're about to find out they owe more than they can pay. Someone sitting in a waiting room before a medical appointment might be frightened. Someone checking into a hotel after a delayed flight might be at the end of their rope.

You don't know. You can't know.

So the default position is always this: treat the person in front of you as someone who might need a little more care than they're letting on. Not in a way that's intrusive or over the top — just with genuine warmth, proper attention, and the understanding that your reaction to them might matter more today than it usually would.

A quiet spot to sit if they seem overwhelmed. A glass of water if you can offer one. A bit of gentle small talk to fill the wait and ease the silence. Eye contact. A real smile — not a professional one.

Every single setting has the potential to make someone anxious. Anxiety doesn't belong to healthcare. It belongs to being human.

Section 3

How would you feel if…

Before we go any further, try this. Read both scenarios and be honest with yourself about how each one would feel.

Scenario A

You arrive for your appointment. You've had a long journey. You're not sure you're in the right place but you think so. You approach the desk.

The receptionist is head down, typing. You stand there. No eye contact. You wait a few seconds — it feels longer. A colleague comes out from a back room and starts talking to the receptionist about something internal. A delivery, a rota, something. They're now having a conversation. You're still standing there. You don't know whether to interrupt. You don't know if they've seen you. You've been at the desk for nearly a minute and nobody has acknowledged you exist.

How does that feel?

Invisible. Unsettled. Like an inconvenience. And you haven't even started your appointment yet.

Scenario B

Same situation. Long journey. Not totally sure you're in the right place.

You approach the desk. The receptionist is on the phone but looks up immediately — makes eye contact, gives a brief, warm nod that says I've seen you, one moment. Finishes the call within thirty seconds. "Sorry about that — good morning. Who are you here to see today?"

Uses your name when you give it. Tells you roughly how long the wait is. When a colleague comes out from the back, the receptionist says "just give me a sec" and keeps their attention on you until you're settled in a seat.

How does that feel?

Completely different. Calm. Looked after. Expected. Nothing extraordinary happened — just basic human acknowledgement, done consistently.

That gap — between Scenario A and Scenario B — is entirely within your control. That's what this module is about.

Section 4

The four moments that define the experience

Most of the work happens in four specific moments. Get these right and everything else follows.

01
The arrival

The most important moment in the visit. They don't need an immediate full conversation — but they need to be acknowledged. Eye contact, a nod, a brief "I'll be right with you." Something that says: I've seen you. You matter.

02
The wait

People fill silence with anxiety. Tell them how long. Check in if it's running over. Offer something. A client who waits ten minutes feeling looked after has a completely different experience to one who waits five minutes feeling ignored.

03
The handover

Almost always done badly. A vague gesture towards a corridor is not a handover — it's a dismissal. A good handover uses names, connects the two parts of the experience, and makes them feel expected.

04
The departure

The last thing someone experiences shapes what they remember about the whole visit. A rushed departure leaves people feeling processed. A warm, unhurried close is remembered long after the appointment itself.

Section 5

Where it goes wrong

These are the patterns that damage the experience. Most of them don't come from bad intentions — they come from being busy, distracted, or just never having been shown a better way.

The invisible arrival

Someone walks in. Two or three staff are at the desk. Nobody looks up. They hover awkwardly for thirty seconds before anyone acknowledges them. Thirty seconds feels like three minutes when you're standing there not knowing what to do.

Finish what you're doing first

You're in the middle of something. Someone arrives. You hold up a finger or say nothing and keep typing. You'll be with them in a moment — but the message they receive is that whatever is on your screen matters more than they do.

The generic greeting

"Take a seat, someone will be with you." No name. No estimate. No warmth. Doesn't make them feel expected or welcome — just processed and parked.

The unexplained wait

Running fifteen minutes behind and nobody has told the waiting area. People are checking their watches, wondering whether to say something. All of this is avoidable with one sentence.

The internal conversation takeover

A colleague comes out from the back and starts talking to you while someone is standing at your desk. You turn to engage with the colleague. The person at your desk is now invisible again — to both of you.

The cliff-edge handover

"She's ready for you — down there on the left." The person walks into a corridor alone, unsure which door, slightly anxious. Nobody introduced them. Nobody said anything to the person they're going to see about who was coming.

The transactional departure

Payment processed, next person already being called — all in under a minute. They leave feeling like a number. Nothing about that ending makes them want to come back.

Section 6

How it should be done

These are specific things to say and do. Not scripts to read word for word — but language that works and feels natural once it's yours.

Acknowledging arrival

Even when you're busy, acknowledge within seconds.

"Hi — I'll be right with you"

While finishing what you're doing, with eye contact.

"Good morning — do you have an appointment today?"

Warm. Uses the visit as an opener rather than demanding a name.

"Morning Jane — take a seat, [name] is just finishing up. Won't be long."

Uses their name, sets an expectation, names the person they're seeing, removes the anxiety of not knowing what happens next.

Managing the wait

If the wait is running over five minutes, tell them before they have to ask.

"Just to let you know, [name] is running about ten minutes behind — sorry about that. Can I get you a drink while you wait?"

"It's a bit of a wait, I'm afraid — are you okay there? Can I get you anything?"

For someone who seems anxious during the wait. You've seen them. That matters.

The handover

Walk them to the door where possible. Use names.

"[Name], this is Jane — she's here for her 11 o'clock."

"Jane, this is [name] — you're in good hands."

Brief, warm, personal. It costs fifteen seconds.

The departure

Don't rush it. Close the interaction properly.

"All sorted — your next appointment is booked for [date]. Hope that wasn't too stressful!"

"Hope everything goes smoothly with the move."

If something notable happened — acknowledge it. This is the thing that turns a client into someone who sends their friends.

Section 7

Making it personal

This is what separates a good front-of-house from a great one.

📋
The pre-arrival scan

Glance at the diary before each appointment. If you're confident you know who's walking towards you — greet them by name before they've reached the desk. If there are two or three it could be, hold back. When you're confident, use it.

✏️
The note habit

When someone mentions something personal on the way out — a holiday, a new job, a big event — make a brief note on their record. Going to Thailand — two weeks. Fifteen seconds to write.

💬
The callback

Next time they come in: "Jane — how was Thailand?" Three words. Jane feels known. Jane tells her colleague about it on Monday. That's how loyalty is built.

Loyalty isn't built through schemes or discount codes. It's built through the simple act of remembering that the person in front of you is a person.

Section 8

Your environment

Before any of the above can land, the space needs to work. Your front desk communicates something to every person who walks in before anyone opens their mouth.

You may not be able to change everything. But notice it.

  • Can someone see a member of staff clearly the moment they walk in — or is the desk hidden, obstructed, or hard to find?
  • Is there anything on the desk that shouldn't be visible — confidential documents, personal items, yesterday's lunch?
  • Is the waiting area calm enough for someone to feel settled — or noisy, chaotic, or overlooked?
  • Do people know where to go when they arrive, or are they guessing?
  • Is there a quieter spot available for someone who seems overwhelmed or needs a bit of space?

You're not responsible for fixing the building. But you are part of the environment — and you're the part that can change today.

Activity

The 60-second audit

Before you move on, do this

Next time you arrive at work, walk in through the front entrance as if you've never been there before. Stand where a visitor would stand.

  • What do you see in the first ten seconds?
  • What do you hear?
  • What does the desk look like from where you're standing?
  • Does anyone acknowledge you — and how quickly?
  • How does it feel?

Write down three things you notice below. You'll return to these in the reflection.

Case Studies

Two stories worth reading

🏨
Case Study 1

The hotel

A five-star resort. Guests arriving after long journeys — some international, some clearly exhausted, some visibly stressed after delayed flights or difficult connections.

One particular member of the front desk team had a habit. Before each arrival, she'd check the reservation notes. If a guest had emailed ahead, or if anything was noted about their journey, she'd have it in her mind. When a couple arrived late in the evening after a seven-hour delay, she didn't wait for them to reach the desk and explain themselves.

"Mr and Mrs Clarke — we've been expecting you. That sounds like a really difficult journey. Let me get you checked in and I'll have something warm sent up to your room."

She used their names. She acknowledged what they'd been through. She removed every bit of friction from the next five minutes.

What happened

The Clarkes mentioned her by name in their review. They came back the following year and asked for the same room. Nothing about the hotel had changed. The building was the same. The room was the same. What changed was thirty seconds at the front desk.

⚖️
Case Study 2

The law firm

A client arriving at a solicitor's office for an appointment about a family matter — a difficult one. The kind of appointment people dread for weeks beforehand.

At Reception A, the experience was efficient. Name taken, visitor badge issued, seat indicated, water offered. Professional. Correct. Entirely forgettable.

At Reception B — same firm, different office — the receptionist noticed the client looked pale and was gripping her bag tightly. She didn't ask what was wrong. She just said:

"Take your time — there's no rush. I'll let [solicitor] know you're here. Can I get you a cup of tea?"

She found a quieter corner of the waiting area, away from the main door. She checked back once, briefly, before the appointment started.

What happened

The client later told her solicitor it was the first time in weeks she'd felt like someone was actually looking after her. The receptionist had no idea what the case was about. She didn't need to. She just read the room and responded to what she saw.
